Research area

A diversity-driven investigation about the ecologically, biotechnologically and evolutionarily important group of bacteria; Planctomycetes. Though ubiquitously present, much of this phylum is under-sampled and unexplored. This phylum also consists of an intriguing group of bacteria which are capable of the anaerobic ammonium oxidation (anammox), a process which can be looked forward for its potential use in sustainable removal of fixed nitrogen. The anammox reaction is an anaerobic conversion of substrates, ammonia and nitrite into dinitrogen (Figure 1). Compared to the conventional methods, the anammox process is economically and environmentally beneficial as it requires less energy, low oxygen, does not need an external electron donor, and reduced sludge production. An in-depth study of the diversity and ecology of this group of bacteria would help to elucidate their use in wastewater treatment. A significant population of Planctomycetes is known to be associated with eukaryotic hosts especially algae. Thus, algae represent a potential candidate for the discovery of new taxa as well as to add more knowledge to the ecology, diversity and interactions of Planctomycetes. Though the interaction with algae and Planctomycetes is well known, the diversity of anammox bacteria associated with algal groups is not well studied. Hence, knowledge about the anammox activity of the Planctomycetes associated with algae as well as the diversity of anammox bacteria linked to the algae itself (which is hitherto unknown) could pave way for a sustainable and energy-efficient technology for the treatment of ammonia-rich environments. The overarching goal of the project is to better understand the diversity of Planctomycetes and to explore the active microbial participants in effective nitrogen removal and to design a potential green technology mission to clean ammonia-rich wastewaters.
